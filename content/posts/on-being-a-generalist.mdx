Sometime in my third year as an engineer, I had a minor crisis. Everyone around me seemed to be picking a lane — distributed systems, ML infrastructure, compilers, security. I was still writing backend code one week, wrangling some data pipeline the next, occasionally touching the frontend because nobody else wanted to.

I felt like I was falling behind. Spreading too thin. Becoming mediocre at everything instead of excellent at something.

I was wrong. It just took me a few more years to realize it.

## The specialist trap

Specialists are valuable. Deep expertise is irreplaceable in the right context. If you're building a database engine, you want someone who has spent a decade thinking about storage formats and query planners.

But most software isn't a database engine. Most software is a collection of tradeoffs between different systems, different teams, and different requirements that evolve faster than any one person's mental model.

This is where generalists shine.

## What generalists actually do

A generalist doesn't know a little about everything. A generalist knows *enough* about many things to:

- See connections that specialists miss
- Translate between domains (product ↔ backend ↔ infra)
- Identify when a problem has already been solved in a different field
- Move fast in new territory without waiting for a specialist

The best generalists I know aren't jacks of all trades. They have real depth in two or three areas, with genuine breadth across many more. T-shaped, but the vertical bars are real.

## The thing nobody tells you about specialization

Here's the dirty secret: deep specialization compounds faster early, but plateaus faster too.

As a specialist, the first few years feel like rocket fuel. You go from junior to senior faster. You become *the person* for a specific class of problem. That's real and valuable.

But as systems grow more complex — as they always do — the edges between domains become the hardest problems. The specialist who can't see past their domain becomes a bottleneck. The generalist who can hold multiple contexts simultaneously becomes the glue.

```
Early career:     Specialist >> Generalist
Mid career:       Specialist ~= Generalist  
Senior/Staff:     Generalist >> Specialist (for most orgs)
```

## Practical advice

If you're a generalist worrying you're spreading too thin:

1. **Pick two things to go deep on.** Doesn't matter what. Depth anywhere builds the muscle for depth everywhere.
2. **Read outside your domain.** Distributed systems papers if you're a frontend engineer. UX research if you're a backend engineer. Cross-pollination is your superpower.
3. **Say yes to the weird project.** The data migration nobody wants. The internal tool with no clear owner. The legacy system that needs refactoring. You'll learn more in three months than a year of comfortable work.

The industry will always ask you to specialize. Sometimes the most interesting answer is: *not yet.*
